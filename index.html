Evaluating a Website

Anyone can create a website and put anything on it. It is up to the viewer to determine whether the information is reliable, accurate, truthful, well-researched, and meaningful. You will need to be able to evaluate websites where you gather information for various inquiries in this course. You may also find these criteria helpful when accessing information in your day-to-day life.

The following are some criteria that can be used to evaluate a website:

The Source of the Information

      • What is the domain of the website?
      • What does the domain tell you about who produced the website e.g., government, educational institution, organizational or commercial?
      • Who is the author of the information?
    The Credentials of the Source
      • Investigate the background information of the author or organization that created the website.
      • Check the "about us" page - does it exist?
      • Is there contact information? Does it “make sense?
    Objectivity and Bias
      • Is the purpose of the website to promote a belief or agenda?
      • Is there advertising that influences the content?
      • Is the point of view or position taken clearly stated and why?
      • Is the bias appropriate?
    Currency of the Information
      • Check when the website was created and when it was last updated.
      • Are the links current and working?  e.g., The Toronto Star - notice that the date and time when the website was last updated are indicated
    Coverage
      • Is the topic covered in depth?
      • Are there links to other similar websites or further details?  e.g., The Lung Association - notice that there are links to other professional societies
    Verifiable
      • Is there a bibliography or works cited on the website?
      • Can you find two or more reliable sources that provide the same information? e.g., Doctors Without Borders - provides an extensive resources section in the News and Resources section.
    Design and Ease of Use
      • Are the grammar and spelling correct?
      • Is there a site map?
      • Is there a search function available?
      • Is the site easy to navigate and logically arranged? e.g., David Suzuki Foundation – this website is easy to navigate

    In this unit, you've learned what a civic issue is and explored various examples of civic issues in local, national, and international communities. You also now know how to distinguish a fact from an opinion - a very important skill when investigating issues. Not only have you looked at the contributions of people who are civic role models, you have also spent time reflecting on how some of your decisions and behaviours have also made a difference. Throughout this unit, you have had the opportunity to think about some new civic issues or explore some that we have already skimmed in more detail. You may even have already formed opinions about them. If so, this will be the chance to ensure your opinion is informed by facts! In this culminating activity, you will choose your own issue (on topics such as poverty, violence, pollution, child labour, bullying, racism, lack of funding for government services, taxes, military spending, human trafficking), search for facts, and express your opinion. It's time to speak your mind!

     Stage 1 - The Proposal

    Select a civic issue that you would like to investigate and answer the following questions. When you have finished, you may submit them to your teacher for approval. It is acceptable to choose an issue about which you already have an opinion. If you are unsure of ideas or a direction in which to go, you should consult with your teacher. Do not move forward with your inquiry until your teacher has approved your chosen issue.

    1. The issue I want to use for this culminating activity is:
    2. I want to explore this issue because: (Reason for investigation? Why is this important to me?)
    3. Two questions I have about my issue are:
    4. One source of information I have already found that is related to this issue is:

    Once your issue has been approved, you can move on to the research stage

     Stage 2 - Gathering Factual Information

    Use your inquiry skills to:

    • find factual evidence that you can use to support your opinion. This could be statistics, examples, case studies, or definitions;
    • find at least three different sources. Be sure to make note of these sources for the information that you find; and
    • choose your top three strongest facts and put them in order, starting with your strongest.

    Use this research organizer to complete this stage, and when you've completed it, submit to your teacher for feedback.

     Stage 3 - Evaluation of Sources

    When researching a topic, it's very important to evaluate the sources of your information. In our “Digital Age and “Age of Information,” how can you be sure that the information you locate on the web is reliable?

    At this stage of inquiry, it's important for you to step back and think about evaluating your sources of information. Visit the Minds On section above to learn more about evaluating the quality of your web-based resources.

    Complete this checklist to evaluate at least one of your sources, and submit it to your teacher for feedback.

     Stage 4 - Communicating Your Information

    Now that you have chosen your top three facts and checked your sources, it's time to choose the way you would like to communicate your information by using one of the six options from the choice board below. Complete the task of your choice, and submit your work to your teacher for evaluation. Be sure to check the rubric for this task to be clear on how your work is going to be evaluated.

    CHOICE BOARD OPTIONS (select one):

    Write a three-paragraph report that includes: a) a description of the civic issue b) facts relating to the issue and c) your opinion about the issue.

    Create an info-graphic relating to your civic issue that: a) identifies the issue b) expresses facts relating to the issue and c) shares your opinion. For examples of info-graphics, click here. Your work should include factual data as well as graphics.

    Interview three friends about your civic issue, and then write a speech that compares your opinion to theirs. Your speech should include: a) a description of the civic issue b) facts relating to the issue and c) the opinions of you and your friends about the issue.

    Create a persuasive video or a photo essay expressing your opinion on the issue. Be sure the visual includes: a) a description of the civic issue b) facts relating to the issue and c) your opinion about the issue.

    Write a song or rap that expresses your opinion. In your music be sure to include: a) a description of the civic issue b) facts relating to the issue and c) your opinion about the issue.

    Create a poster aimed at persuading others to share your opinion on the issue. On your poster, be sure to include: a) a description of the civic issue b) facts relating to the issue and c) your opinion about the issue.

     Stage 5 - Documentation

    Using the sources from Stages 2 and 3, plus any additional resources you consulted during Stage 4, you must now complete a proper MLA-style bibliography.

    Need help with this step?  Consider some of the online resources available.  Easy Bib is a resource used by many students or check with your teacher for preference. 

    Once you've completed the necessary documentation, submit your work to your teacher for evaluation.

    Introduction

    In your first culminating activity, you will select a civic issue that interests you, investigate the topic, and critically evaluate your sources. Next, you'll choose one of several different ways to communicate your opinion or point of view as substantiated by the facts. Lastly, you'll document the sources you used in your inquiry. With your eyes now wide open, it's time to express yourself!

    Learning Goals

    By the end of this activity you will be able to:

    • provide an opinion on a civic issue;
    • gather and select relevant evidence on a civic issue; and
    • support your opinion with creditable evidence.

    Success Criteria

    I can successfully:

    • express my point of view on a civic issue;
    • provide factual support for my opinion with relevant evidence; and
    • ensure that my evidence is creditable.

    Curriculum Expectations

    Overall Expectations

    A1. Political Inquiry: use the political inquiry process and the concepts of political thinking when investigating issues, events, and developments of civic importance;
    B1. Civic Issues, Democratic Values: describe beliefs and values associated with democratic citizenship in Canada, and explain how they are related to civic action and to one's position on civic issues.

    Specific Expectations

    A1.2 select and organize relevant evidence, data, and information on issues, events, and/or developments of civic importance from a variety of primary and secondary sources;
    A1.3 assess the credibility of sources relevant to their investigations;
    A1.7 communicate their ideas, arguments, and conclusions using various formats and styles, as appropriate for the intended audiences and purpose;
    A1.8 use accepted forms of documentation;
    B1.4 communicate their own position on some issues of civic importance at the local, national, and/or global level explaining how their position is influenced by their beliefs/values.
